Economic Impacts of Localized Automation in Manufacturing Sectors

v2.1

Submitted by: Dr. Aris Thorne • Dept. of Economics • 2 hours ago

RESUBMISSION DRAFT

Abstract

This paper investigates the localized economic impacts of automation adoption within specific manufacturing hubs in the midwestern United States. While broad aggregate studies suggest a net-neutral employment effect over a ten-year horizon, granular analysis at the county level reveals significant short-term frictional unemployment and wage depression in cohorts lacking secondary technical education. Furthermore, the transition period is marked by an increased reliance on regional safety nets, creating fiscal strain on local municipalities. We propose a localized adaptation framework, emphasizing targeted vocational retraining subsidies deployed concurrently with automation tax incentives, to mitigate these transitional economic shocks.

1. Introduction

The narrative surrounding automation in manufacturing has largely bifurcated into optimistic macro-level productivity forecasts and pessimistic micro-level displacement anxieties. This study seeks to bridge this gap by anchoring the analysis in a highly specific geographic and industrial context. Previous iterations of this research (Thorne, 2021) generalized the impacts across diverse manufacturing sectors. This revised analysis focuses strictly on precision machining and assembly operations within a tri-state area, utilizing a newly available municipal tax database to track indirect economic indicators during periods of rapid capital investment in automated systems.

By isolating these specific variables, we aim to demonstrate that the speed of automation integration, rather than the absolute scale, is the primary driver of localized economic disruption.

2. Methodology and Data Sources

Data was aggregated from state-level employment security commissions and cross-referenced with capital expenditure reports filed under the Regional Manufacturing Incentive Program (RMIP). The primary challenge in this methodology is the differentiation between automation-driven job displacement and displacement resulting from broader macroeconomic cyclicality. To address this, we employed a difference-in-differences (DiD) estimation strategy, utilizing counties with similar industrial compositions but differing rates of automation adoption as control groups.
